Animals

Posted in When the Bell Goes by Elisa on Jul 12, 2020

Brianna Seng is an uptight know-it-all.

 She thinks she’s smarter than everyone, but isn’t anywhere close to the top of our English class. And that’s saying something; we’re in a general stream.

 She’s got an opinion about everything; her hand shoots into the air before Ms Turay has even finished asking the question. Typical. And she’ll probably get the answer wrong.

 “Women are supposed to be caring, so when her mum gets angry it’s like she’s betraying the little girl,” she says simperingly, her hands folding in front of her. She even glances over her shoulder to see who’s heard her enlightening words – as if what she’s saying isn’t totally anti-feminist. Ms Turay nods along, giving the answer way more consideration than it’s worth. She’s a new teacher; young, pretty, and desperate for approval.

 “That’s one way of reading it,” she politely responds. I’ve heard that one before, and everyone knows it’s teacher talk for, “Not really, you moron.”

 “Although we also need to consider the perspective of the person who’s telling the story...” she continues, turning back to the board to add to the branching visual she’s drawn there. It maps out the connections between the different characters we’re discussing, like the concept of family is so alien to us we need to be taught it in school. How this is related to English is beyond me.

 But I’m not paying much attention – my eyes are still on Brianna, who sits a few rows in front of me.

 She’s got this smug look on her face as she leans sideways, into the waiting embrace of Fletcher. Her dainty little shoulder fits snugly under his bicep. I like to say that hate is too strong a word to use on a person, but I think I can make an exception for her.

 A sharp elbow jabs me in the side and I turn to glare at Andi. Her eyes are glued to the front of the class and I follow them to find Ms Turay staring at me. Crap, did she ask me something?

 I duck my head and scribble in my notepad, but the words have no real meaning. I don’t even know what we’re supposed to be writing about. And besides, my mind is elsewhere.

 Fletcher is so smart. Way smarter than Brianna. He’s not like any of the other guys in our year: overgrown apes who barely know a girl from a bong. I don’t know anyone who’s as quick-witted as he is – is it any wonder that he gets distracted in class? Ms Turay scolds him but he just shoots her scornful looks because they both know he’s too clever to be here.  ​​​​ 

 And he’s hilarious.

 

On the first day of school this year Andi and I stood outside the demountable on the edge of the grounds waiting for our new English teacher to turn up. We’d been early, and Fletcher and his friends were the next group to arrive, walking down the gravel path towards us. We heard them before we could see them. Four boys in Nike trainers, all with tiny, slouching backpacks that they surely couldn’t fit anything in. Their voices were raised as they talked over one another, exchanging barbs and laughing. One in particular won out the most often, and was met with guffaws from his friends. It was Fletcher.

 I hadn’t had a class with him since year 8, but I soon discovered that I’d been missing out.

 Unlike his try-hard girlfriend, Fletcher doesn’t waste his smarts on studying and answering pointless questions in class. Instead, he just has fun.

 Really, that day had been Ms Turay’s fault. She’d come in way too hard – it was the beginning of the year and she wanted us to fall immediately and perfectly into line as if we hadn’t just returned to slavery from a major stint of intoxicating freedom. Someone was bound to act up, and isn’t it her job to know that?

 Fletcher just wouldn’t stop talking. At first I thought somehow he hadn’t noticed that the rest of the class was silent. He didn’t even seem to react when Ms Turay said, “Excuse me,” right at him and almost everyone else turned to look. It was like he was in another world. Even the boy he was talking to was glancing at the teacher nervously.

​​  Ms Turay had to find his name out from another kid.

 “Ah,” she said, “Fletcher – I was warned about about you. Would it be okay if I start my lesson now?

 Honestly, it was almost like she was egging him on.

 Fletcher turned slowly towards her, and said, “Sure Miss.”

 Very casual – no fuss.

 “Thank you,” she replied, apparently satisfied. But as she continued talking, Fletcher piped up again. He wasn’t whispering or raising his voice – just speaking at a normal volume.

 Ms Turay, in contrast, was clearly annoyed, and her tone went up a notch as she blustered at him to stop talking. He completely ignored her. His composure was stunning. It was as if she wasn’t even there – like she was invisible. I had to put my hand over my mouth to stop myself from snorting.

 They continued like this – back and forth – her voice demanding to be heard, and his unhurried, as if he had all the time in the world and was not afraid to waste it. The rest of us watched on, quietly awed at the ugly flush on the young teacher’s face, and the cold finesse with which Fletcher served his passive aggression.

 Eventually she asked him to leave the room.

 “Okay,” he said politely. But he didn’t go. He just sat there, as if completely oblivious to the daggers she was staring at him. She asked him to move again. And once more he said he would, before following up with: “In a minute Miss.” Then he turned back to his mate and picked up the conversation where he’d left off.

 She tried to threaten him next, taking out her phone and saying that she’d call the front office to have him removed. At last, Fletcher seemed to break character and a look of hope flickered briefly over the teacher’s ashen face.

 “I’m just sitting here, Miss,” he said.

 “That’s the problem,” she replied. “You’ve been told to go outside.”

 Without warning, he changed tack, appearing indignant.

 “It’s the first day of the year Miss – are you really gonna kick me out? You’re supposed to care about my education.”

 Ms Turay only paused for a moment, before choosing what she clearly thought was the lesser of two evils, “Okay Fletcher, if you want to learn you can stay in class, but you have to be quiet.”

 “Alright Miss,” he said, his tone taking on the quality of someone long-suffering.

 The teacher nodded, and relaxed a little, relieved to have navigated her way out of the standoff.

 She’d turned on her computer then, and projected a slideshow on to the whiteboard. The first slide had the words, ‘Why study English?’ written on it. I groaned inwardly and slouched down into my chair. This was going to be a long lesson. She began to speak.

 But she had only gotten a few words into her practiced spiel when another voice rose to join hers. Unbelievably, Fletcher was talking again. ​​ 

 I’d never really seen a teacher lose it like that before. Explosive anger was something I had definitely experienced, but this was different. She was just frozen. She didn’t know what to do: to keep fighting this losing battle, or to try to ignore it and admit defeat on her first day in front of this class. You could see how torn she was as her eyes darted around – trying to keep up as Fletcher uttered infuriating responses to every request she made. ​​ 

 It was crazy. And she was panicking.

 He was a genius. And I was in love.

 

After class Andi and I walk a few meters behind Fletcher and Brianna.

 As we watch, Brianna reaches for Fletcher’s hand, but he draws it away. Andi grabs my elbow in excitement, and my breath hitches as Brianna turns her face to him in surprise. They pause together. I link my arm through Andi’s, and we draw off the path and sit down at a bench to watch. She knows the deal, and pretends to be searching for something in her bag as I stare over her shoulder.

 The conversation looks tense. They speak in hushed tones so I can’t hear the words, but Brianna is visibly upset, and Fletcher glares at her, his face hard. His mouth barely even moves as he speaks in a measured tone. Finally, she gives him a little shove and walks away. What a psycho.

 I get up as soon as she’s gone, leaving Andi to walk to lunch alone as I approach Fletcher. She’ll understand.

 “Everything okay? She seems pissed!” I say. I’m still a few paces away but Fletcher is turning to leave so I’m forced to call to him. It’s embarrassing, but he still gives me a lopsided grin.

 “She just hates being wrong,” he says.

 I roll my eyes and can’t stop myself from replying, deadpan, “What a surprise.”

 At first I’m worried I’ve offended him, but then he smirks, his cheek dimpling. We lock eyes for a moment, then he shrugs, and walks away.

 I’m sure there’s something between us, and I think he feels it too.

 

Sure enough, when we get to English the next day, Fletcher is sitting in Andi’s seat.

 “Where am I supposed to sit?” she hisses at the helpless look I give her. She’s not really mad – just putting on an act in response to Fletcher’s lazy stare.

 “Next to Brianna,” I grin, and she let’s out an exaggerated sigh before choosing an empty chair in the back row instead.

 As luck would have it, Ms Turay wants everyone to work in pairs and Fletcher complains to me about Brianna. Apparently she’s not only a smart-arse, but also totally possessive.

 “She’ll be paranoid about me sitting with you,” he tells me, and I laugh like I don’t know what he means by that.

 “Why?” I say.

 “She’s just jealous all the time,” he explains. “It’s kind of embarrassing.”

 I nod, “Some girls just have really low self-esteem. It’s sad.”

 Brianna keeps turning around to glance desperately at us, though she’s pretending she isn’t. But there’s only so many times one person can need to stretch their neck within the same one hour period. Fletcher’s right – she is embarrassing.

 

~ * ~

 

It feels like an age has passed before they break up. Fletcher tells me all about it as we watch a movie in class. He seems relieved to be able to get it off his chest – he’s confiding in me, and Brianna practically bristles with anger a few rows in front of us. She looks as if she’s only just resisting the temptation to turn and glare.

 “She’s bat shit,” he says, barely whispering. “I used to love her but she’s obsessed with me. Relationships should be equal.”

 I completely agree. It’s crazy how in sync we are.

 Ms Turay glares at us, but doesn’t yet seem willing to pause the video. Despite the fact that there are several hushed conversations going on in the class, there will still be an uproar, and she looks tired. The lesson has that end-of-day sparkle about it; everyone is on tenterhooks, anticipating the bell that will signal the start of the weekend.

 “You’re out of her league anyway,” I say to Fletcher, moving my arm to rest on the table in what I hope is a casual position. ​​ 

 “You’re right,” he replies simply.

 He puts his hand on the desk, next to mine. Our pinky fingers are nearly touching.

 

Later, he asks Andi and I if we’re going to the train station. We are, so a group of us join the steady stream of students who walk there after school: Andi, me, Fletcher and his friends.

 The pair of us normally walk alone, and it’s a strange feeling to be amongst this group of boisterous boys. Other kids look at us, and they clear a larger path to let us through. I feel kind of big and important.

 As we turn the final corner and the station comes into view, we see the 3:10 train pulling away. An old man who appears to have just missed it stamps his foot and curses, even though the next one will be here in only six minutes. He is balding with deep frown lines on his face, and wears a long brown coat as if to spite the heat. He turns to glare as more kids spill up on to the platform around him from the street. A couple of Fletcher’s mates start making dumb jokes about old people and nudging each other.

 They’re still half-heartedly rough-housing as we get to the top of the concrete ramp. Fletcher leads the way to the ticket machine, and we follow him as he passes close by the old man. His friends nearly bump into him as they jostle each other, and the man looks like he’s going to have an aneurysm.

 “Excuse me!” he bristles, as we walk away from him, his voice sounding strained by the apparent effort of speech.

 The boys all stop and turn around to look at him.

 “What?” one of them demands, sticking his chin out.

 “Watch where you’re going!” the old man hisses, his wrinkled face scrunching further as he scowls at them.

 “You were standing in the way,” Fletcher retorts, quick as lightning.

 “So you push me onto the train tracks, eh?”

 Fletcher laughs, “You’re still up here, aren’t you?”

 “It was a near thing,” the man snaps.

 “Look mate,” Fletcher says, grinning and glancing around at his smirking friends, “if we wanted to shove you onto the train tracks you’d be mince meat by now.”

 I guess that’s kind of funny, but I don’t really get why he’s bothering with this guy.

 “You’re threatening me!” the man accuses, increasingly angered by the boy’s laughing faces. He’s almost shaking now. This is getting stupid.

 “Fletcher-” I say, but he puts his hand up to stop me, a tiny frown flickering over his face. And I do stop – just like that – my mouth snapping shut almost before I can register what I’m doing. I glance sheepishly at Andi, and find she’s already looking at me, aghast. She knows how much I hate it when people tell me to be quiet, but something about his look shook me. He is so cooly confident, and I’m not sure what to think.

 “You kids don’t respect anyone!” the man is saying now, spittle flying from his mouth. “You walk around like you own the place.” ​​ 

 “It’s hard to respect someone so close to death,” Fletcher scoffs. There’s a little venom in his voice that makes me feel tense. True, this guy’s kind of a jerk, but he’s just some old dude. His life’s probably sucky enough as it is.

 “Why don’t you come here and say that, if you’re such a big man?” comes his reply, and I feel a niggle of worry. ​​ He doesn’t seem like he’d be much of a match for four teenage boys, but I really don’t want to get caught up in a fight over nothing.

 Fletcher’s lip curls up and the sun glints off the point of one of his canines. It makes me squint, and in that moment, I see a hint of the animal in his face. He’s deadly calm; an alpha male lying in wait for the charge of a challenger. His tone isn’t violent, but it contains the threat of it nonetheless, and my heartbeat begins to increase. I feel frozen – powerless.

 “Let’s just relax,” says Andi, moving closer to the man while her eyes stay on the boys. She’s taken the words straight out of my mouth – the ones that I’m suddenly too stricken to say – and tries to calm everyone in that careful, considered way she has. I love her for that; she always knows what to do when I don’t.

 But, much to my chagrin, everyone just ignores her.

 “You’re really gonna fight a kid?” says Fletcher, his tone bitingly sarcastic. “Get a life.”

 “Makes no difference to me how old you are,” the man says. “I’ll give you the hiding your parents should have.”

 “Ooo,” say several of the boys in unison – Fletcher included. They grin stupidly round at each other; a family of monkeys hooting their assent.

 “Guys, come on,” I find my voice at last, even if it does come out a little whiny. I’m not entirely sure what I’m going to say – I don’t want to be a total buzz-kill – but it turns out not to matter anyway. Fletcher interrupts me.

 “Calm down,” he says, scowling at me. “We’re just messing around.”

 I hate being told to calm down almost as much as I hate being told to be quiet. I can feel the colour rising in my cheeks – and it doesn’t help that Fletcher appears totally at ease. While he’s clearly not bothered at all, my mouth is dry and the words feel stuck in my throat.

 I am mute. I don’t know what’s wrong with me. A million different come-backs run through my head but I can’t quite grasp at the correct one. I barely register the noise of the 3:16 train approaching, or the sound of the man shuffling off to meet it further up the platform.

 With a withering glance in my direction, Fletcher turns to leave. This is my last chance. I can’t stay silent.

 “Arsehole!” I blurt at his retreating back.

 “Psycho,” he says over his shoulder as he gets on the train.

 And there’s not really anything I can say to that which won’t prove him right. My blood boils and – mortifyingly – I feel the tell-tale hot prickle of tears in the corners of my eyes. Andi, beside me, squeezes my arm.

 “Come on Luce,” she says quietly, and leads me to a carriage further down the train.

 

On Monday morning Fletcher is sitting next to Brianna again. She looks back at me with an upturned nose – like I should be jealous that’s she’s back together with that waste of space.

 Unbelievable. Some girls are so stupid.
