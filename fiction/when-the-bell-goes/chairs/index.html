Chairs

Posted in When the Bell Goes by Elisa on Sep 19, 2020

There are only two rules for getting away with almost anything: pretend to be sincere, and always be polite. Adults are simple creatures, and they love that stuff.

 

The exception is probably your parents. They just know you too well.

 

I crunch absently at my cereal, my left hand resting on the remote, and listen to Mum walk down the hall towards the stairs. The third step creaks, and I press the button to turn on the TV. Her voice reaches me almost immediately.

 “Turn it off!” Mum calls, from upstairs now, “We agreed.”

 “I didn’t agree...” I mutter, turning the volume down till I can barely hear it.

 Mum clatters down the stairs and is back in the room before I can catch more than a few sentences from the cartoon cat on-screen. She’s holding her laptop charger and a stack of marking I assume she took to bed with her last night.

 “Off! Off!” she says, extending her hand toward me. I snatch the remote out of her reach.

 “I’m doing it,” I say, taking way too long to scrutinize it, before switching the TV off.

 “I’m not driving you if you miss the bus,” Mum tells me, continuing on her morning route around the house.

 “That’s fine – I’ll just stay home,” I say. She rolls her eyes before disappearing into the kitchen.

 “You’ll walk,” she replies.

 I shovel in the last few mouthfuls, then pause to stare my silent resentment into the sweetened milk left in my bowl. I wanted toast but there was no bread in the freezer.

 “Don’t forget your detention,” she’s back.

 “How could I?” it’s rhetorical.

 “Don’t be a smart arse. I don’t want another call from the school.”

 “Yeah, cause you’re practically swimming in those.”

 “One’s enough,” she looks at me pointedly, as if I’m supposed to respond.

 “Yes, okay,” I whine, “I guess I’ll postpone my bomb threat till next week.”

 “Not funny,” she says, glancing at her watch, “It’s ten to. You’d better shift it.”

 

 

School’s easy. Not the work – though that’s not so bad either – but the whole process of being there. Maybe you think that’s a dumb thing to say, but it seems to me that so many people find just being at school so hard. Don’t get me wrong. I fully admit that sometimes the other kids are jerks, or the lessons are boring, or the teachers are stupid, but getting through the day without being bothered isn’t such a huge task. Staying out of trouble is easy. It sounds hypocritical, given the detention I’ll serve after school today, but I wasn’t trying to stay out of trouble when I got that. I guessed what would happen and I did it anyway; I made a choice. Some people don’t seem to be making any choices at all.

 

Nathan Bell is standing on a chair.

 “Nathan,” Ms Gardiner is speaking through her teeth, “Get down now.”

 I’m pretty amazed that he’s managing to keep his balance. This isn’t one of those regulation plastic, stackable school chairs with a back and a low centre of gravity. The home economics rooms have high stools.

 Nathan ignores her, turning 180 degrees on one foot. The stool wobbles precariously but he sticks his long arms out and gains his balance, smirking the whole time. Impressive.

 I can tell that Ms Gardiner doesn’t know how to respond to this. Nathan is the only boy in this class, and judging by the look she’s giving him – one of mortified fascination, like a scientist who’s just discovered a new species of flesh-eating bacteria – he may be the only boy she’s ever taught. He’s a new addition, and to say that he’s integrating poorly is an understatement.

 According to Lucy, who sits beside me staring daggers of contempt in Nathan’s direction, he was kicked out of his metalwork class for “dangerous behaviour”. Apparently he’d held a blow torch near his butt and pretended to fart on it – classic gag. I get that moving him away from heavy machinery was probably a good idea, but I don’t think giving him access to sewing needles is the best alternative. Though, granted, he hasn’t shown much interest in those so far.

 Nathan hops down from the chair, after waiting until an appropriate amount of time has passed since the request was made. But he doesn’t sit. He starts to wander round the class. As he passes our bench, he turns a couple of dials on first my, then Lucy’s sewing machines. Hers starts to burr with activity, and she huffs as she hurries to turn it off. Lucy likes making things with her hands – she’s the reason I’m taking this elective really – and she clearly doesn’t appreciate the minor ruckus Nathan’s causing. I’m not too bothered. My pencil case project is not looking very pencil-case-like, and I was ready to pack it in before the period even started.

 Ms Gardiner’s at her desk now, with her phone in her hand. Nathan notices her long enough to say, “No phones in class Miss!” before getting his own out. It’s her turn to ignore him.

 “Mr Pascal – Nathan Bell is refusing to sit down and disrupting the class,” she says, the phone to her ear now, “We’d like some assistance please.”

 I don’t know who “we” is. Maybe her and Lucy.

 Nathan’s pretending to take a phone call himself now. And they say teachers aren’t inspiring.

 Ms Gardiner instructs us to ignore him, turn on our machines and take out our work. We probably could have done this ages ago, but there’s a strict ‘no electricity without express teacher permission’ policy in the Home Ec rooms.

 Mr Pascal arrives soon after we’ve started sewing, his chunky mustache bristling with self-importance as he sweeps into the room. He greets Ms Gardiner and she nods her head in the direction of the trouble-maker as if he’s hard to spot sitting on a desk top, legs swinging dramatically.

 “I thought we were going to have a good day today, Nathan,” Mr Pascal says. Patronizing. And there’s that word again: “we”.

 “I am sir,” says Nathan, “but I can’t speak for you.”

 Mr Pascal frowns but I think it’s a pretty good line. Nathan’s kind of funny. I’ve never had a class with him before – we’re in different streams I guess – but I’d always assumed he was a moron.

 “It’ll be detention in a minute Nathan,” Mr Pascal says, pointing his finger at a chair as if to say ‘put your bum on that’.

 “But I’ve got training today sir!” Nathan says, his eyes wide for a moment before he recovers himself.

 “Not if you don’t sit down and behave yourself,” Ms Gardiner pipes up helpfully. Nathan scowls at her as he sinks into a chair at last. She looks smug. I thought I liked her, but I might be changing my mind. The two teachers smile and thank each other. Lame.

 

I’m surprised to see Nathan in the detention room. So surprised that I forego one of the empty seats towards the front to join him in the back row.

 “I thought you got out of detention,” I say to him. He looks at me sideways from his slumped position. He seems flatter than he did when I saw him earlier; he looks tired.

 “What?” he says.

 “In sewing – I thought they didn’t give you detention.”

 At the front of the class, Mr Pascal looks up from his book, “Andrea...” he warns me.

 “It’s Andi,” I say as if on autopilot.

 “Andi,” he amends, “no talking.”

 I sit back in my chair and shrug. Nathan waits until Mr Pascal is looking away again.

 “That was period one,” he says, “I got this in period four.”

 “Nathan,” Mr Pascal’s tone is sharp now, “Move away from Andi please.”

 “But she sat next to me!” Nathan protests.

 “Sorry sir,” I cut in, “It’s my fault – I asked him a question about maths. We’re allowed to do homework in detention, aren’t we?”

 I get out my notebook and a pen as Mr Pascal nods.

 “Can we work together sir?” I ask respectfully, “I’ve been really struggling to figure out these index laws and I think Nathan could help me.”

 “I don’t-” Nathan starts to say and I kick him under the desk. He scowls but shuts his mouth. Mr Pascal looks at me skeptically.

 “Unless you could help me sir?” I continue, “Do you know anything about quotients?”

 I doubt he does. Mr Pascal was a Phys Ed teacher before he became a deputy. He seems mildly alarmed.

 “As long as you stay on topic,” he concedes, casting his eyes back down to his novel.

 “So what did you do?” I whisper to Nathan, leaning over my page as if I’m looking at my notes. He doesn’t even bother to pretend.

 “None of your business,” he says.

 “I wagged the assembly on Friday to join the year 9 reward period. Took nearly the whole hour before someone realised I was from the wrong year group, but I got a free ice-cream out of it,” I tell him.

 “That’s dumb,” he says.

 “And standing on a stool is the mark of a genius?” I retort, rolling my eyes.

 “Get stuffed,” he mutters, turning away from me.

 “Fine,” I say.

 I pull my notebook closer and start to doodle in the corner. I draw a vine that curls its way up the side of my page. It’s soon dotted with tiny flowers in various stages of growth. The ones at the top have unfurled their petals and reach for a sun that does not yet exist.

 “I kicked a chair,” Nathan says eventually, breaking the silence. I knew he’d crack.

 “What is it with you and chairs?” I say, “Are they symbolic or something?”

 He ignores the questions and answers one I didn’t ask.

 “I hate English.”

 “And?” I say, “What’s that got to do with it?”

 “Nothing,” he replies, “I just got mad. So I kicked it.”

 “What about training?” I ask.

 “I’m not at training,” he grouses, “Obviously.”

 I roll my eyes again. He’s pissed, but it’s clearly his own fault.

 “Here’s an idea...” I say slowly, as I carefully draw tiny little leaves, “Why don’t you try not kicking things?”

 Nathan doesn’t answer but he folds his arms across his chest. The over-sized sleeves of his jacket crinkle with the movement. He seems to sink even further into his chair. He’ll be on the floor in no time at this rate.

 “You know, if you don’t do anything, teachers don’t bother you,” I tell him matter-of-factly.

 “Maybe they don’t bother you,” comes his reply, “but they always have a go at me – even when I’m not doing anything wrong.”  

 “And that happens a lot does it? You not doing anything wrong?” I feel kind of pleased with myself when he doesn’t reply, but then I catch a glimpse of my reflection in the window above his head. I look just like Ms Gardiner this morning: smug. I wipe the grin off my face.

 “Try it on Thursday,” I say, more sincerely now, “in sewing. Ms Gardiner’s old but she’s stupid and easy. She never checks our work – I’ve been making the same pencil case all term.”

 Nathan grunts, and puts his hood up, effectively blocking me out.

 

On Thursday I catch Nathan’s eye but he looks away before I can communicate anything significant. Lucy sees him glancing in our direction and huffs.  

 “He’d better not come over here today,” she says, clutching her material to her chest, “I’m nearly done and I bet he’d just love to ruin it.”

 “I don’t think he cares about your apron Luce,” I reply, but she just grumbles as she chooses some thread from the chaotic box of class supplies.

 The lesson begins and I throw a couple of looks at Nathan. He sits despondently on his stool, staring out the window and fiddling with a switch on his sewing machine: the presser foot descends with a clunk, then ascends with a click. ​​ After about ten minutes, Ms Gardiner picks her way over to him.

 “Nathan,” she says, and he looks up at her with narrowed eyes, “Do you want to try some sewing?”

 He mumbles something and she says she can’t hear him.

 “Don’t know how,” he repeats himself, barely audibly.

 “Well of course not – but I’ll show you,” she says brightly, “That’s what I’m here for.”

 She picks out a bobbin for him and he takes it in his big hand. She takes another bobbin for herself and demonstrates how to use the machine to wind thread onto it, put the bobbin in its case, and the case in the compartment.

 “Easy enough,” she says, and he nods reluctantly. She moves away from him, taking her pre-threaded bobbin with her and dropping it back in the box. Nathan looks at the tiny metal wheel as he holds it between two fingers, then drops it clumsily onto the winder. I watch as he manages to get the thread on it, and he seems to be rather enjoying pressing on the foot pedal and watching it spin in response. He even gets the bobbin into its cover on the second go, his tongue between his teeth as he concentrates on not losing the end of the thread. The trouble comes when he tries to get it into the compartment. It slips from his grasp and clatters to the floor. Ms Gardiner looks up.

 “Do you need my help Nathan?” she calls from across the room. Heads turn to look at him.

 “No,” he says, grabbing the bobbin from the floor and trying again. He stoops low before his machine, fiddling with the tiny metal cover and trying to fit his fingers in the small compartment. He drops it again, and it conspicuously rattles over the lino in the silence of the work room. This time, instead of picking it up, he stares at it as if it’s insulted him. I’m just about to go over and do it for him when Ms Gardiner speaks again.

 “Now Nathan,” she says, “We’re not going to have a repeat of Monday, are we?”  

 Nathan looks murderous, and I don’t really blame him. It’s so uncalled for.

 “I’m trying Miss, but it’s not working,” he grinds out, “This thing’s broken or something.”

 “It’s not broken Nathan,” she says, “You just need to be more careful.”

 “I am being careful,” he replies.

 “If you need my help-”

 “I don’t need your help!” he interrupts, “I don’t want to sew anyway. This sucks.”

 “Watch your language,” she snaps.

 “What language? I didn’t even swear.”

 “If you can’t behave appropriately in the classroom I’m going to have to ask you to step outside,” she says this in a measured tone, like an actor reading lines. Nathan stands suddenly, and his chair topples to the floor with a crash behind him.

 “That’s it,” says Ms Gardiner, pointing to the door, “You can wait outside for Mr Pascal.”

 “I’m already going,” he says, storming out.

 Ms Gardiner reaches for her phone and my hand shoots into the air. I speak before she calls on me: “Can I get a drink Miss?”

 She waves me out as she dials.

 I plop down onto the bench next to Nathan. He looks deflated, like an old party balloon that fell behind the couch and was forgotten.

 “Nice work,” I say.

 “I told you. She wouldn’t leave me alone.”

 “You’ve got to go longer without drawing attention to yourself,” I reply.

 “How long?” he spits on the floor in front of him. Gross.

 “Until they’re not looking at you anymore,” I say, as if it’s obvious. To my surprise, he laughs.

 “So, never,” he says. I start to reply but the classroom door swings open and Ms Gardiner pokes her head out.

 “Inside,” she directs this at me.

 “Sorry Miss,” I say, “Just tying my shoe.”

 I stand up and sidle past her.

 

I see Nathan in the line for the canteen. He’s finishing eating a cheese toasty and it looks as if it’s fortified him: he’s bouncing restlessly as he waits.

 “You’ve been here before,” I say, nodding at his greasy hand.

 “I want another,” he replies.

 A moment later, he scowls at the nice volunteer who tells him they’ve run out of hot food.

 “Fucking year 7s,” he says, “They always eat everything good.”

 The lady behind the counter frowns as she passes me my chocolate milk, but she doesn’t say anything. Good move.

 “Canteen food sucks anyway,” I tell him as we walk away, “I’d kill for a hash brown – a McDonalds one, not the soggy ones they serve here.”

 “Don’t,” he says, clutching his stomach, “I’m starving.”

 I snort and he gets this strange smirk on his face – like he’s just spotted a chair that he wants to kick over.

 “Since you’re so into wagging,” he says conversationally, “What have you got last period?”

 “Why?” I say, looking at him sideways.

 “I’m thinking about getting some food.”

 I snicker, “Mum would kill me...”

 

Mr Pascal does not enjoy being a disciplinarian. Or at least, that’s what he tells us as he escorts us back onto school grounds. He sends Nathan to class but leads me to his office. I sit across from him and watch as a little crinkle of concern appears on his forehead.

 “I’m disappointed to see you in here again so soon Andi,” he says. I resist the urge to tell him that he shouldn’t have invited me in then. Mum’s definitely going to hear about this, and I’m on damage control. I hang my head.

 “Sorry sir,” I mumble.

 He lectures me about duty of care and the effect that missing school can have on my learning – like skipping period five history might mean the difference between me getting a PhD or ending up homeless. ​​ 

 Finally, he says, “I didn’t know you and Nathan were friends. You don’t seem the type.” I don’t even want to know what that’s supposed to mean. Adults are so sick.

 “We all have to make choices,” he continues. “We can choose our friends and we can choose how we respond to different influences… We can choose how to act.”

 “I made a bad choice,” I say, picking up on the theme, and he nods slowly like it’s the saddest thing he’s ever heard, “But I can see that now. I won’t make the same mistake again.”

 He just keeps looking at me expectantly so I ask, “Can I go back to class now?”

 “I’ll be calling your mother,” he says lamely, “to discuss consequences.”

 He turns to his computer and I guess that means I’m dismissed. I wait a beat just in case. Just to be polite. And then, for some reason – maybe to bookend the conversation – I ask: “Do you want me to get Nathan?”

 “No thank you,” says Mr Pascal, clicking at something on his screen. I’m suddenly suspicious. So suspicious that I forget about the delinquent’s code: snitches get stitches. Or snitches fall in ditches. Or snitches are... big jerks?  ​​​​ 

 “Nathan was there too,” I blurt out, “He made the same bad choice as me.”

 He doesn’t even glance up as he says, “Nathan can’t help himself – he’s restless. But you should know better.” ​​ 

 “Maybe I’m restless too,” I say. I don’t know why I’m still having this conversation. I guess I just want to know what he’ll say. That’s always my problem: too curious for my own good. ​​ ​​ 

 Finally, Mr Pascal turns his eyes to me. He looks at me for a long moment, his expression is a picture of practiced blankness. I stare back, waiting.

 “I know you know how to behave yourself,” he says eventually.

 I can feel my lip curling at this. Why does he think he knows this about me? He only found out my name last week.

 “Don’t make that face at me Andi,” he says, as if to prove how well he’s learnt it. You’d think he’d be used to people giving him the stink-eye in his line of work.

 

I’m only half way through my maths homework when mum’s phone rings. It’s taking me ages to finish and these are the exact same problems we did in class today. I barely got them then, even with Mrs Adair standing over my shoulder and drilling me. I don’t know how I’m supposed to do them by myself.  ​​​​ 

 I can hear the murmur of conversation from the next room. Mum has her measured, teacher voice on – the one she reserves for complaining helicopter parents and waiters who mess up her order. This can’t be good.

 “I’m not even going to ask what you were thinking,” she says, from the doorway. Her arms are folded in front of her chest and she purses her lips at me.

 “Okay,” I say. That was easy.

 “Two calls from school within a fortnight,” oh, she’s not finished, “And Mr Pascal says you gave him cheek.”

 “You always say I’m cheeky,” I reply.

 “He’s a deputy,” she says, as if that’s supposed to make a difference, “And you know better.”

 Mr Pascal had said the same thing, and it seems off to me. Obviously I didn’t know better. Or maybe I did, but I didn’t care. What’s the difference? Either way, mum seems determined to make me care.

 I feel bad, as she lectures me, but I can’t really do anything about it besides apologize. And I do, several times, but she still doesn’t seem satisfied. It’s embarrassing, she says, to get phone calls from the school.

 “You didn’t do it,” I say, shrugging.

 “But your behavior reflects poorly on me,” she replies, “You need to think about how you’re effecting the people around you.”

 I make a face. I wasn’t really thinking much of anything when I did it, and besides, I don’t think it’s nearly as big a deal as she’s making it out to be. It’s not like I was caught giving blowjobs in the media lab. Her disapproval itches at me, and I squirm under her gaze.

 “Fine,” I say, “I get it.”

 She frowns, “Watch your tone.”

 “This is just what my voice sounds like!” I don’t know what she wants from me.

 “Finish your homework,” she snaps, “And you can forget about going to the shops this weekend.”

 Fine by me, I think. Nathan and I bought enough Mars Bars to last a month in solitary confinement.

 

A whole week passes before Nathan approaches me. He finds me after school as I stand by the road, waiting for mum to pick me up. He grabs my phone straight out of my hand and looks at the screen as I splutter in protest.

 “Boring,” he says, handing it back to me before I blow my top. I roll my eyes.

 “What were you expecting?” I say.

 “You were staring so hard at it – I thought you might have nudes on there or something,” he teases, before realising what he’s accidentally implied.

 “Nudes of myself?” I say, rubbing it in, and now it’s his turn to splutter.

 “No! I just meant-”

 I decide to put him out of his misery: “Whatever perv.”

 Nathan casts his eyes about, spotting a low limestone wall and moving to jump onto it. He walks the length, kicking fallen honkey nuts from his path as he goes.

 “Got plans this weekend?” he says. I shake my head.

 “Gonna lie low for a bit,” I tell him, “Mum’s been super testy.”

 “That’s smart,” he says, and I give him a small smile. I know it is, but it’s kind of nice to hear it from someone else.

 Nathan paces back and forth. He picks up a stick and we both watch as he flicks it into the rose bushes with a swish. It clatters about between the branches before settling amongst the thorns.

 “Are you getting picked up?” I ask him, and he shrugs. Whatever that means.

 He’s just wrestling a larger stick from the garden bed when I see Mum’s car pull up. I grab my bag and move off.

 “See ya,” I say over my shoulder, and Nathan looks up, brushing the floppy hair from his eyes and depositing a smear of dirt on his forehead. I laugh as I wave and get into the car. He raises his hand in response.

 “Who was that?” Mum asks as we pull away from the curb.

 “Nathan,” I tell her. She doesn’t look surprised. We drive in silence for a moment, and she bites her lip.

 “I’m not sure I want you hanging around that boy,” she says finally – reluctantly. Even she knows it’s a messed up thing to say.

 “Why?” I demand.

 “I’m worried about you.” It’s not really an answer.

 “Well, lucky for you, I’m fine.”

 “You know what I mean,” she says. Her eyes flicker rapidly from the road, to me, and back again. Surely that’s not safe.

 Of course, I do know what she means. I’ve read enough good-kid-goes-wrong-with-the-help-of-a-bad-influence novels. They must be on the English curriculum; required reading for idiot teens. But what offends me is that she thinks the sentiment applies to me. My whole life she’s told me what a strong, independent woman I’m becoming, but now she seems to have conveniently forgotten about that. Thanks for the vote of confidence, Mum. In the silence, she contemplates, and her face gets sad.

 “Sometimes I don’t know who you are anymore,” she says at last, “I can’t predict what you’re going to do.”

 My chest tightens and I swallow thickly. This is getting into dicey territory – I’m in danger of feeling sorry for her. I try for misdirection.

  “But no phone calls this week Mum,” I say in a simpering voice that I somehow know will annoy her. That did the trick: she frowns in response.

 “I’m not going to congratulate you for doing exactly what you’re supposed to, Andi.”

 “Jeez,” I mutter under my breath, “What happened to ‘positive reinforcement’?”

 She hears me – of course she does – and we pull up to a red light just in time for her to turn her head and pin me with a stare.

 “You’re not a little girl anymore,” she says, “I won’t protect you from the consequences if you don’t start taking responsibility for yourself.”

 “I never asked for your protection,” I say, and even I grimace a bit at the way it comes out.

 “You don’t get the luxury of asking for it,” she snaps, pressing a little too hard on the accelerator when the lights change, “You have it already.”

 We lurch away from the line as I fight the urge to point out that she’s just totally contradicted herself. I get the feeling that this conversation is over.

 

Outside the sewing room, I say hi to Nathan. Lucy, who’s hurrying to finish her Diet Coke before the period starts and is in a much better mood today, does the same. He says hi back, and makes a joke about caffeine that falls spectacularly flat. To cover his embarrassment, he moves to put one foot on the bench that lines the corridor, but it’s at this moment that Ms Gardiner flings open the door. Distracted, he misses his footing and stumbles. I guffaw, and even Lucy cracks a grin.

 I can feel Ms Gardiner’s beady eyes on us as we recover ourselves and file past her. She really needs to get a grip.

 Inside, we take our usual seats and begin preparing our work stations. I get my charger out of my bag and plug my phone in at the wall along with my sewing machine. Ms Gardiner is behind me before I even realise she’s moved.

 “Hand it over,” she says, her palm flat and extended towards me.

 “What?” I say, genuinely surprised.

 “You know the rules,” she tells me, “And you’ve just broken two in a row.”

 I’m shocked. I’ve plugged my phone in to charge nearly every sewing lesson this term and she’s never had an issue with it before – I don’t think she even noticed. When I don’t move, she reaches around me and wrenches it out of the socket with way more force than is necessary.

 “What the hell?” I say, standing up. My stool screeches obnoxiously. ​​ 

 “I’m sick of students thinking my class is just a free ride,” she tells me. I open my mouth to tell her she should probably teach something other than Home Ec then, but Nathan beats me to it.

 “Even I get more warnings than that,” he says, from across the room. He’s leaning against a bench, casually fiddling with his own phone. He’s not even using it, just playing with the cover. Ms Gardiner looks like she’s going to explode.

 “I’ve had enough of this,” she tells no one in particular, her voice tight, “I won’t be putting up with any rubbish from anyone today. We’ll work in silence. We’ll have no phones. We’ll have no disruptions.”

 We we we. Adults love that word. It’s the kind of word that suggests something shared. But she and I have nothing in common and I feel resentful of the implication.

 “Now,” she says, fighting to keep her cool, “everybody sit down and get to work.” ​​ 

 She looks around the room, as if daring us to disagree. She makes eye contact with Nathan – registering his scowl – and then a moment later, with me. I stare back, my face hard, but I don’t move. No one does. The air is thick with tension and I realise that everyone is waiting. For what, exactly, I don’t know.

 Ms Gardiner begins to narrow her eyes at me and I mirror her. My mind races. I know exactly what I should do. I know the moves that will calm her down. I can see myself in my mind’s eye, ducking my head in mock-embarrassment and smilingly docilely in the face of her disproportionately heated disapproval. Instead of matching her contempt, I would murmur, “Sorry Miss,” and take my seat, quietly feigning interest in what is possibly the ugliest half-made pencil case in the world, until she moved on.

 But I don’t do any of these things. Because they don’t feel right – they don’t feel true – and I’m done faking nice for this bitch.

 

I refuse to sit down.
